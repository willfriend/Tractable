Social Media is inflaming conflict

.

How do we put out the fire?

From polarization to ethnic violence,
From the United States to Sri Lanka,
Social Media is intensifying
intergroup conflict.

Behavioral scientists have documented dozens of psychological and cognitive mechanisms that drive intergroup conflict. Unfortunately, the same features of social media that keep users scrolling and ad revenue flowing - content algorithms and echo chambers - also exacerbate these psychological mechanisms.

Fortunately, there is over a decade's worth of scientific research on techniques for overcoming these psychological barriers to conflict resolution. Our mission is to leverage social media to apply these techniques at scale in intergroup conflicts around the globe.

To start, we're targeting we've deemed the 'Core Four' barriers that are driving intergroup conflict in the United States:

1. Partisanship
2. Intergroup Prejudice
3. Naive Realism
4. Competitive Victimhood

Content + Conflict Resolution

We create content - video, visual and audio - that incorporates rigorously tested methods for overcoming psychological barriers to conflict resolution.

Partisanship

Paradoxical Thinking increased support for compromise and engagement among rival groups across multiple experiments. We're creating a paradoxical thinking campaign for political partisanship.

Prejudice

Messages about group malleablility, heterogeneity and counter-stereotypes all decrease intergroup prejudice. We're applying these techniques to racial, political and cultural prejudice.

Naive Realism

is a cognitive bias which hampers peoples' ability to see events from others' points of view. Applicable to a variety of US conflicts, this bias can be reduced through simple awareness.

Competitive Victimhood

is the tendency for groups to deligitimize outgroup suffering and emphasize ingroup suffering. It can be attenuated through outgroup recognition and re-categorization . We're creating content that incorporates these techniques to promote cooperation across group lines.
